Project 1. How the brain perception multidimensional biological motion (BM) attributes

Publication

Wang, R., Lu, X., Jiang, Y. (2023). Distributed and hierarchical neural encoding of multidimensional biological motion attributes in the human brain. Cereb Cortex.
https://doi.org/10.1093/cercor/bhad136

Research Background

Since the point-light biological motion (BM), which isolates human body movements, was introduced by Johansson, alarge body of research has revealed that the human visual systemcan efficiently extract and process distinct physical, biological,and social attributes from BM, such as facing directio, gender, and emotional state. Despite that the ability to recognize and evaluate different BM attributes has been well demonstrated at the behavioral level, it remains largely unknown how the brain simultaneously encodes distinct attributes from BM.

Figure 1.1 The stimulus of BM

What analysis did i do

1. performed multiple regression representational similarity analysis (RSA) and multi-voxel pattern analysis (MVPA) to identify the brain network in response to each BM attribute.
2. introduced the scrambled representational dissimilarity matrices (RDMs) in RSA to explore whether the brain areas identified by RSA were specific to the neural encoding of BM attributes.
3. analyzed the correlations between behavioral results and neural responses by performing an RSA with behavior RDMs.
4. tested which regions are primarily involved in the integration process of the BM attributes using psychophysiological interaction (PPI) analysis.

Figure 1.2 Stimuli, task, and RDMs.

Results

These results together demonstrated that the respective neural encoding of the facing direction, gender, and emotional state information embedded in BM stimuli involved considerably overlapping brain regions, which raised the possibility that the neural representations of the three attributes of BM might be shared and possibly interact with each other.

For IN (Imagery of Non-speech), decoding accuracy was significantly above chance in bilateral PPC but not in vlPFC nor in the cingulo-opercular network.

Two-sided paired t-tests showed that the decoding accuracy in parts of PPC (left intraparietal sulcus and right superior parietal lobule) was significantly higher in IN than that in IS reliably across searchlight radii, suggesting memory representations in PPC.
